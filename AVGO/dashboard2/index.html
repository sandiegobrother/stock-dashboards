Revenue Analysis
Broadcom's revenue story is one of disciplined compounding through strategic M&A. The base semiconductor business grew at ~15% organically (FY2021-FY2023), then the landmark $69B VMware acquisition in FY2024 vaulted total revenue to $51.6B — a 44% single-year leap. Crucially, this was not a one-time boost: VMware's subscription conversion contributed $21.5B in FY2024 and is growing as ~⅔ of renewals are converted to recurring subscriptions. The AI semiconductor segment is the second growth engine, growing from negligible to $12.2B in FY2024 and ~$21B in FY2025 (+77% YoY). The combination of AI silicon momentum and sticky VMware subscriptions creates a revenue base that is both fast-growing and increasingly predictable. The 5-year CAGR of 28% places Broadcom among the fastest-growing large-cap technology companies globally.

Net Income Analysis
Broadcom's net income trajectory is a tale of two lenses. GAAP net income dipped in FY2024 to $5.9B due to ~$12B of non-cash VMware intangible amortization — a predictable accounting consequence of large acquisitions that overstates the economic decline. Non-GAAP net income, which strips out amortization, grew every single year: from $12.6B (FY2021) to $31.2B (FY2025) — a 148% increase over five years. The FY2025 GAAP recovery to $25.5B (44% margin) demonstrates that as amortization charges decline, GAAP earnings are converging back toward the non-GAAP reality. The FY2025 GAAP net income of $25.5B is a record and represents one of the highest net income figures of any semiconductor company in history. Net income CAGR (non-GAAP) over 5 years: approximately +20% annually.

Free Cash Flow Analysis
Free cash flow is the single most important metric for evaluating Broadcom's financial health — and it tells an unambiguous story of compounding excellence. FCF has grown every single year without exception, from $13.8B in FY2021 to $26.9B in FY2025. The FY2024 FCF margin dipped to 38% because of the large VMware integration costs and elevated interest expense from acquisition debt. FY2025 saw margin recovery to 46% as VMware synergies materialized. This level of FCF generation — nearly $27B annually — funds Broadcom's entire dividend program ($10B+), aggressive debt repayment ($15B+ since VMware close), and share buybacks, simultaneously. Hock Tan's capital allocation discipline is evident: every dollar of FCF is being deployed with surgical precision to maximize long-term shareholder value. The FCF conversion rate (FCF as % of non-GAAP net income) averages 85%+ — indicating extremely high earnings quality.

Margin Analysis
Broadcom's FY2025 margins are extraordinary by any measure. A 76% gross margin, 73% adjusted EBITDA margin, and 44% GAAP net margin place it in the top tier of all public technology companies globally. The FY2024 margin dip (gross margin to 68%, GAAP net margin to 11%) was entirely driven by the VMware acquisition accounting — specifically, the write-up of deferred revenue that was immediately amortized, compressing reported gross margin. This is a well-understood, temporary accounting artifact, not an economic deterioration. By FY2025, gross margins recovered to 76% — an all-time record — as the deferred revenue headwind normalized and VMware's high-margin software subscription revenue began flowing through fully. The 73% adj. EBITDA margin is particularly remarkable: it means that for every $1 of revenue, Broadcom retains $0.73 before capital expenditures — a reflection of the asset-light semiconductor design + software business model.

Debt Analysis
The $69B debt taken on to finance the VMware acquisition was the largest technology M&A deal ever — and the skeptics were wrong to worry. At FY2024 close, ND/EBITDA stood at 1.9x. By FY2025, it had already declined to 1.3x — driven by $43B in adjusted EBITDA dwarfing the interest burden. With interest coverage above 10x and $26.9B in annual FCF, Broadcom's debt is entirely manageable and rapidly declining. The debt-to-equity ratio of 0.80x is below the technology sector average. Context matters: this is $65B in gross debt for a company generating $43B EBITDA and $27B FCF annually — the debt-to-FCF ratio is just 2.4x. Management has guided to return net leverage to below 1x within 3 years of the VMware close. Debt is a non-issue at Broadcom's current earnings trajectory. The current ratio of 1.9x signals strong near-term liquidity.

ROE & Capital Allocation Analysis
Broadcom's ROE trajectory reveals the mastery of capital allocation under Hock Tan. ROE rose from 25% (FY2021) to a peak of 59% (FY2023) — driven by expanding margins and aggressive buybacks. The FY2024 drop to 9% was entirely mechanical: the VMware acquisition massively increased the equity base and simultaneously compressed GAAP net income through amortization charges. The FY2025 recovery to 33% reflects both the normalization of these charges and the growing earnings power. ROIC of 21% demonstrates that acquisitions are generating returns well above Broadcom's cost of capital. Most critically, Hock Tan has returned ~$56B to shareholders over 5 years through dividends and buybacks while simultaneously paying down $15B+ in acquisition debt and investing in R&D — a feat of capital allocation discipline that is virtually unmatched in the semiconductor industry. The quarterly dividend has been raised consistently, growing at a ~17% CAGR over the past five years.

Is Broadcom Financially Strong or Weakening? — Definitive Assessment
Broadcom is financially among the strongest companies on the planet — and the trend is firmly toward further strengthening, not weakening. The 5-year financial record is unambiguous: revenue grew 2.1x, free cash flow grew from $13.8B to $26.9B, non-GAAP net income grew 148%, and the company returned ~$56B to shareholders while simultaneously completing and integrating the largest software acquisition in history.

The only apparent "weakness" in the 5-year record — the FY2024 GAAP net income and ROE compression — is entirely an accounting artifact of VMware amortization, not an economic deterioration. Non-GAAP metrics show continuous, uninterrupted improvement across every year. The $65B debt load, while large in absolute terms, is rapidly being extinguished by $27B+ in annual FCF and is already below 1.5x EBITDA — well within investment-grade territory.

Looking forward, the AI semiconductor franchise ($21B in FY2025, growing 77% YoY) and the VMware subscription annuity ($21.5B in FY2024, ~⅔ converted to subscriptions) create a dual-engine compounding machine that is accelerating, not decelerating. Management's FY2026 guidance implies revenue approaching $90B — which, combined with continued margin discipline, should drive FCF toward $35B+. Hock Tan's track record of capital allocation discipline over 15+ years gives high confidence that this trajectory will be maintained. Financial health grade: A+. Trend: Strengthening.
